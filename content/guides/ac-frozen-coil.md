You open the closet, peek in the utility room, or step outside and you see it — ice on the refrigerant lines or on the indoor unit itself. In Florida heat, it feels wrong, and it is. A frozen coil means your AC cannot cool your home properly, and leaving it running while frozen can damage the compressor — the most expensive part of the system. The good news: most of the time this is something you can diagnose and fix yourself before ever calling a tech.

<div class="callout c-warn">
<span class="c-title">⚠ First thing to do right now</span>
<p>Turn the system off at the thermostat immediately. Set the fan to ON (not Auto) so the blower continues to run and helps thaw the coil. Do NOT try to chip or scrape the ice off — you will damage the fins. Let it thaw on its own. Depending on how frozen it is, this takes 1–4 hours.</p>
</div>

## Why Does a Coil Freeze?

Your evaporator coil works by absorbing heat from the air flowing over it. Two things can cause it to freeze:

<div class="callout c-success">
<span class="c-title">Cause 1 — Restricted airflow (most common, usually DIY-fixable)</span>
<p>When not enough warm air moves across the coil, the coil gets too cold and the moisture in the air freezes on contact. The usual culprit is a dirty or clogged air filter. Other causes: blocked or closed supply vents, a dirty coil itself, or a failing blower motor.</p>
</div>

<div class="callout c-danger">
<span class="c-title">Cause 2 — Low refrigerant (needs a licensed tech)</span>
<p>Low refrigerant causes the pressure inside the coil to drop too low, which makes it run too cold and freeze. You cannot add refrigerant yourself — it requires a licensed HVAC technician with EPA 608 certification. If this is the cause, there is also a leak somewhere in the system that needs to be found and repaired, not just topped off.</p>
</div>

## After It Thaws — Work Through This Checklist

Once the ice is fully melted (no frost is visible and the drain pan has water in it from the melt), do the following before restarting:

<div class="steps">
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">1</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Replace the air filter — no matter what</h3><p>Even if you changed it recently, pull it out and look at it. A filter that looks lightly used to the eye can still have enough restriction to cause a freeze in Florida's humidity. If there's any doubt, put a new one in. This costs $5–$15 and solves the problem the majority of the time.</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">2</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check every supply and return vent in the house</h3><p>Make sure every supply vent is fully open and unobstructed. Check every return vent (the larger grilles that pull air in) as well. A common mistake is closing vents in unused rooms to "save energy" — this actually restricts airflow to the system and can directly cause a freeze.</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">3</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check the drain line and drain pan</h3><p>When a coil freezes and thaws, it produces a significant amount of water — more than normal operation. Make sure the condensate drain line is clear so all that meltwater can drain out. If the drain pan overflows, you risk water damage. Use a shop vac on the outside drain opening if you suspect a clog (see our drain line post for full instructions).</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">4</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Look at the evaporator coil itself</h3><p>Turn off / verify off the breaker to the air handler first. Once thawed, open the air handler access panel and look at the coil. Is it visibly dirty — caked with gray dust, lint, or debris? A dirty coil restricts airflow just like a dirty filter. Cleaning an evaporator coil properly requires coil cleaner spray and some patience — we have a separate guide on that. A very dirty coil may need professional cleaning. Be mindful that looking at the coil from the side of the blower fan is the exiting side of the evaporator coil. The dirt and obstructions will be on the underside of the coil opposite of the blower (the blower fan pulls air across the evaporator coil).</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">5</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Restart the system and monitor it for one hour</h3><p>After confirming a clean filter, clear vents, clear drain, and that all panels are back on the air handler: turn the breakers back on and set the thermostat to Cool. Let it run for a full hour. Check the air handler — is it blowing cold air? Is the outdoor unit running with the fan spinning? After 45–60 minutes, go back and check the lines going into the air handler. If frost is starting to form again, stop the system. You likely have a refrigerant issue.</p></div></div>
</div>

## When to Call a Tech

<div class="callout c-danger">
<span class="c-title">🛑 Call a licensed tech if</span>
<ul>
<li>The coil freezes again after you replace the filter and clear the vents</li>
<li>The filter was clean when you pulled it (points to refrigerant or airflow issue beyond the filter)</li>
<li>You see oil staining or residue near refrigerant line connections (sign of a leak)</li>
<li>The blower fan inside is not spinning at full speed or making noise</li>
</ul>
</div>

## A Word on Refrigerant

I see a lot of homeowners get talked into a "refrigerant recharge" as the first thing a tech does on a service call. If your system is properly sealed and maintained, it should not need refrigerant added. **Refrigerant doesn't get "used up" like gas in a car** — it circulates in a closed loop. If the level is low, there is a leak. Topping it off without finding and fixing the leak is a temporary fix that will have you calling again within a season.

That said, there is a practical exception. If a leak is very small and has slowly lost refrigerant over several years, a periodic top-off can sometimes be the more economical choice. Tracing a tiny leak can take a tech hours of billable time, and depending on the age of the system and what they charge these days, simply maintaining the charge every so often may cost you less than the hunt. Ask the tech to weigh the cost of finding the leak against the cost of just keeping it topped off, and decide based on the numbers and how many years you plan to keep the system.

A reputable tech will find and repair the leak before recharging. If they want to just add refrigerant and leave without addressing the source, get a second opinion.

## Common Terms Used in This Guide

<div class="callout c-terms"><dl class="gloss">
<dt>Evaporator coil</dt><dd>the cold coil inside the air handler that absorbs heat from your indoor air.</dd>
<dt>Refrigerant</dt><dd>the sealed chemical that circulates through the system to move heat. Often called by the old brand name "Freon."</dd>
<dt>Compressor</dt><dd>the pump in the outdoor unit that moves refrigerant through the system — the most expensive part to replace.</dd>
<dt>Blower motor</dt><dd>the indoor fan motor that pulls air across the coil and pushes the air through your ducts.</dd>
<dt>Condensate drain line</dt><dd>the PVC pipe that carries condensation water to the outside.</dd>
<dt>EPA 608</dt><dd>a federal certification a technician must hold to legally handle refrigerant.</dd>
</dl></div>
