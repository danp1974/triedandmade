Your air conditioner is running — you can hear it, the fan is blowing — but the house just won't cool down. In Florida, that's not just uncomfortable, it's a real problem. Before you pick up the phone and schedule a service call (which starts at $100–$150 just to show up), run through this checklist. In my experience, roughly half of all "not cooling" calls turn out to be something the homeowner could have handled themselves in under 15 minutes.

<div class="callout c-warn">
<span class="c-title">⚠ Quick safety note before you start</span>
<p>You do not need to cut the power just to run through this checklist — leave the system on for the steps below.</p>
<p><strong>But any time a step tells you to remove a panel, or to reach behind or inside one, shut off BOTH breakers first</strong> (the indoor air handler and the outdoor unit). Anything behind a panel can expose you to live electrical parts and moving fans.</p>
<p>Also stop right away and cut both breakers if you ever smell burning, see sparks, or notice a sweet chemical smell (that can be refrigerant). In those cases, do not touch the unit — call a tech and step away from the area.</p>
</div>

## The 8-Step Checklist

Work through these in order. Most people find the problem within the first four steps.

<div class="steps">
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">1</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check the thermostat settings</h3><p>This sounds too obvious, but I cannot count how many service calls I showed up to where the thermostat was set to HEAT instead of COOL, or the fan was set to ON instead of AUTO. When the fan is set to ON it blows air continuously — even when the system isn't actively cooling — and that warm air feels like a broken AC when it isn't. Set the mode to Cool, the fan to Auto, and make sure the setpoint is actually below the current room temperature. (Once the house is comfortable again, some people prefer to run the fan on ON full-time — see the side note just below the checklist.)</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">2</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check your circuit breakers — both of them</h3><p>Your AC system has two separate breakers — one for the air handler inside and one for the condenser outside. Even if you have a single unit outside (a package unit, sometimes called a mobile home unit), it may still have more than one breaker, or a separate breaker for the heat strips. A lot of homeowners only check one of the breakers. It's important to understand that the indoor unit usually supplies the low-voltage (24-volt) control signal that tells the outdoor unit's computer board when to run — so if the indoor breaker is off, the outdoor unit may not start even though it has its own power. Go to your electrical panel and look for any breaker in the middle position (not fully on, not fully off). Sometimes a breaker can look like it is not tripped even when it is, so you will not hurt anything by turning off both breakers anyway. Reset by pushing fully OFF first, then back ON. Important: if it trips again immediately, stop — that's an electrical fault that needs a licensed tech.</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">3</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check (and replace) the air filter</h3><p>A clogged filter is one of the most common reasons an AC runs but doesn't cool effectively. Pull the filter out — it's usually in the return air vent (a large louvered grille in a hallway ceiling or wall) or at the air handler itself. If it looks dirty, replace it now. In Florida, with our heat and humidity, filters can clog in as little as 3–4 weeks during peak season. Be careful what filters you buy — the most expensive is not always the best, and one that is so dense that it reduces too much airflow isn't the best either. The best way to test a filter is to hold it out flat in one hand and sprinkle table salt on it; if the salt falls through, it's not a good filter for your system. A clogged filter suffocates the system and can also lead to a frozen evaporator coil (see Step 5) or poor drain line flow (see Step 7).</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">4</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check that all supply vents are open and unblocked</h3><p>This is mostly for homes with vents in the floor. Walk through the house and make sure every supply vent (the ones blowing air out) is open and that furniture, rugs, or curtains aren't blocking them. Restricted airflow makes the system work harder and cool less. Also check that no return vents are blocked — returns are the larger grilles that pull air in. Blocking those starves the whole system.</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">5</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check for a frozen evaporator coil</h3><p>Go to your air handler (the indoor unit — usually in a closet, attic, or garage). Look at the large refrigerant lines going in. If you see ice or frost anywhere on the unit or the lines, your coil is frozen. Turn the system completely off and set the fan to ON to thaw it — this takes 1–3 hours. The most common cause is restricted airflow, usually a dirty filter (which you just checked). Once thawed, replace the filter and try again. If it freezes again, the issue is more complicated and needs to be evaluated by a tech.</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">6</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check the outdoor condenser unit</h3><p>Go outside and look at your condenser (the big unit outside). Is the fan on top spinning? If the unit is humming but the fan isn't turning, you likely have a failed capacitor — a cheap part but one that needs a licensed tech due to the voltage involved. Also check: is the unit buried in leaves, grass clippings, or overgrown shrubs? The condenser needs airflow on all sides. Before you rinse it, shut off the breaker or pull the disconnect to the outdoor unit — never spray a unit that still has power to it. Then gently rinse the fins with a garden hose if they're visibly clogged with debris. A best practice is to spray the coil down, but only in a manner that gets wet only the parts that would normally get wet when it rains.</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">7</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Check the condensate drain line</h3><p>This is a big one in Florida. Your AC removes humidity and drains that water out through a PVC pipe — usually to the outside near the condenser. If this line gets clogged (and in Florida, algae builds up fast), a float switch will shut the system down entirely to prevent overflow and water damage. Find where your drain exits at your home. Using a shop vac on the end of the drain line outside will often clear a clog in 30 seconds. Pour a cup of white vinegar into the drain access port, if you have one, monthly to help keep it clear. Also, as I mentioned in Step 3, a dirty filter or a filter that is too restrictive can cause a drain line to act as if it is clogged. Basically, if there is too much air restriction, the blower motor creates a negative pressure on the drain line and actually turns it into a straw, trying to suck air from the outside. This causes water to stay in the drain pan or line and makes the float switch turn off the unit. (The deeper cause is usually a drain line that lacks a proper P-trap on the negative-pressure side of the blower; the trap is what stops the blower from pulling air back through the drain. Restriction from a dirty filter just makes it worse.) This type of issue usually causes the unit to run for a while, then shut off on overflow, which stops the blower. When the blower stops, the negative pressure releases and the water drains out on its own. About five minutes later, the outside unit starts up again, and the whole cycle repeats.</p></div></div>
<div class="step"><div class="step-num">8</div><div class="step-body"><h3>Do a full system restart</h3><p>After working through the above: turn the thermostat completely off, go to the breaker panel and shut off BOTH AC breakers (usually marked A/C, Condenser, A/H, or Heat), wait a full 5 minutes, then turn the breakers back on and set the thermostat back to Cool. This is not the same as just pushing buttons on the thermostat. The 5-minute wait lets the system pressure equalize, which protects the compressor and resets any nuisance lockout features of the condenser control board. After restart, give it 15–20 minutes before deciding it's not working.</p></div></div>
</div>

<div class="callout c-info">
<span class="c-title">Side note — about leaving the fan on "ON"</span>
<p>Once your house is comfortable again, some people prefer to run the fan in the ON position full-time. In Florida homes with vaulted ceilings, hot air collects up high, and running the blower continuously keeps air moving so the system does not have to work as hard to hold the set temperature. The blower only draws about 3–6 amps — roughly one to two ceiling fans. There are trade-offs to running it this way (humidity is the main one), so I'll cover the full pros and cons in a separate article.</p>
</div>

<span class="c-title">✓ Did one of those steps fix it?</span>
<p>Great — make a note of what it was and set a reminder to do seasonal maintenance before next summer. Most of these issues are completely preventable with a basic once-a-year checkup.</p>

## When to Stop and Call a Tech

None of the above is a substitute for a licensed technician when the situation calls for one. Stop the checklist and make the call if any of these apply:

<div class="callout c-danger">
<span class="c-title">🛑 Call a tech immediately — do not keep troubleshooting</span>
<ul>
<li>You smell something burning or electrical coming from the unit</li>
<li>You notice a sweet chemical odor near the system (possible refrigerant leak)</li>
<li>The breaker trips again after you reset it</li>
<li>The coil freezes again after fully thawing and replacing the filter</li>
<li>The outdoor fan isn't spinning and the unit is making a loud hum</li>
<li>Water is actively overflowing from the drain pan (possible hole in the pan)</li>
</ul>
</div>

## If You Do Call a Tech — Tell Them This

Come prepared. It saves time, prevents unnecessary upsells, and shows a tech you know your system. Tell them:

<div class="callout c-terms">
<ul>
<li>Which steps from this checklist you already ran through</li>
<li>Whether the coil was frozen, drain was clogged, or filter was dirty</li>
<li>Whether the outdoor fan was spinning when you checked</li>
<li>How long the problem has been happening and whether it's constant or intermittent</li>
</ul>
</div>

A good tech will appreciate that you already did the basics. And if they come out and "fix" something you already confirmed was fine — you'll know to ask questions.

## A Note on Florida Specifically

Most national HVAC content is written for climates that get a few months of summer. We run our systems 10–11 months a year down here. That means filters need changing more often, drain lines clog faster, and condensers work harder. The drain line step above causes more "system shutdown" calls in this state than almost anything else.

If you're maintaining a home that sits empty part of the year — snowbirds, rentals — get someone to check that drain line at least once a month while the system is running. A clogged drain in an empty house is how you come home to a water-damaged ceiling.

## Common Terms Used in This Guide

<div class="callout c-terms"><dl class="gloss">
<dt>Air handler</dt><dd>the indoor part of your system (often in a closet, garage, or attic) that holds the blower fan and the cooling coil and pushes air through your ducts.</dd>
<dt>Evaporator coil</dt><dd>the cold coil inside the air handler that pulls heat and humidity out of your indoor air.</dd>
<dt>Condenser</dt><dd>the outdoor unit that releases the heat your system removed from the house.</dd>
<dt>Capacitor</dt><dd>a small electrical part that gives the motors their starting kick. A failed one is cheap but must be replaced by a licensed tech.</dd>
<dt>Supply vent</dt><dd>a vent that blows cooled air out into the room.</dd>
<dt>Return vent</dt><dd>the larger grille that pulls room air back into the system.</dd>
<dt>Condensate drain line</dt><dd>the PVC pipe that carries the water your AC removes from the air to the outside.</dd>
<dt>Float switch</dt><dd>a safety device that shuts the system off if the drain backs up, to prevent water damage.</dd>
</dl></div>
