My AC Shut Off and Won't Come Back On — How to Check and Clear a Clogged Drain Line

June 6, 2026Before You Call, Maintenance, Florida Specific

AC shut off and won't restart? In Florida it's often a clogged condensate drain line. Here's how to find and clear it in about 15 minutes.

If your AC suddenly shuts off completely and won't restart — no error on the thermostat, breakers look fine, nothing seems obviously wrong — there's a good chance your condensate drain line is clogged. In Florida, this is one of the most common service calls I went on. The combination of humidity, heat, and a system that runs almost year-round creates the perfect environment for algae to build up inside that drain line and block it completely.

The system has a float switch in the drain pan that acts as a safety shutoff (required by code in most installations — especially units in attics or above living space) — when the pan starts to fill because water can't drain, the switch kills the system to prevent water from overflowing onto your ceiling or floor. It's a good safety feature, but it's also why your house is suddenly 85 degrees and you have no idea why.

The fix is usually something you can do yourself in about 15–20 minutes.

First — Confirm It's the Drain

1

Find your air handler drain pan

The air handler (indoor unit — usually in a closet, attic, or garage) sits in or above an auxiliary drain pan. Look for the primary drain pan (it will be inside the air handler). If the drain pan is not visible from the outside of the air handler, first turn off the breaker to the air handler, then remove the front panels to check the drain pan that is inside the air handler. Is there standing water in it? Even half an inch of water is enough to trigger the float switch. If the pan is dry, a clogged drain may not be the issue — go back to the main AC checklist.

2

Find the float switch

The float switch is usually a small device (about the size of your thumb) inside the drain pan or on the drain line coming out of the air handler. It has two wires going to it. When the water level lifts the float, it breaks the circuit and shuts the system down. Some units have secondary drain pans in the attic with their own float switch as well. (Units in the attic with an overflow pan will have two float switches — one on the primary drain pan and another on the secondary drain pan. The secondary drain pan is the pan under the air handler and is larger than the air handler.)

3

Find where your drain line exits the house

Follow the PVC pipe coming out of the air handler. It typically runs to an outside wall near the condenser unit and drips onto the ground. Finding the exit point is important for the next step — clearing the clog.

Clearing the Clog — Step by Step

What you'll need
  • A wet/dry shop vac (this is the most effective method by far)
  • A cup of plain white vinegar
  • A rag or towel
1

Turn the system off at the thermostat

Don't try to clear the drain while the system is running. Turn it completely off first.

2

Put the shop vac on the outside drain opening

Go to where the drain exits the house. Seal the shop vac hose against the opening as tightly as you can — you want suction, not air gaps. A rag wrapped around the connection helps create a seal. Run the shop vac for 30–60 seconds. You will often hear a pop or gurgle when the clog breaks free. Check the vac — if you pulled out a grey-green sludge, that's the algae buildup. That's what was blocking the line.

3

Find the drain access port and flush the line

Most systems (not all — this depends on how the contractor installed the unit; this drain access port is not required by code) have a PVC cap on the drain line near the air handler — this is your access port. Remove the cap and slowly pour your vinegar in. The vinegar kills the algae and flushes any remaining debris toward the outside. Let it sit for 5–10 minutes, then check the outside end to confirm it's flowing.

4

Confirm the pan is empty and the float switch is reset

Before restarting, confirm the drain pan is empty and dry. If there's a manual reset button on the float switch, press it. If not, the switch should reset on its own once the water is gone.

5

Restart and monitor

Turn the system back on. Within a few minutes it should begin cooling normally. Go back to the drain pan every 15 minutes for the first hour and confirm water is not accumulating again. Water flowing out of the outside drain opening during operation is completely normal and means the line is clear.

Preventing It From Happening Again

This is one of those problems that is almost 100% preventable with one simple habit:

✓ Monthly maintenance (takes 2 minutes)

Pour 1/4 cup of white vinegar into the drain access port once a month. That's it. The vinegar kills algae before it can build up into a clog. Set a phone reminder. In Florida, especially during summer when the system runs constantly, once a month is the right interval. In winter when you're running heat, you can back off to every other month.

Some homeowners also install a float switch alarm that sends an alert to their phone before the system shuts down — these are inexpensive (under $20) and a good investment for a rental property or a vacation home that you're not checking daily.

When to Call a Tech

🛑 Call a tech if
  • You cleared the clog but the pan fills up again within a few hours
  • You can't locate the drain access port or the outside drain exit
  • The drain line runs through walls or the attic and you can't access the clog
  • Water damage has already occurred to the ceiling or floor — document it and call immediately
  • The float switch doesn't reset and the system won't come back on after the pan is empty

Common Terms Used in This Guide

Air handler
the indoor unit that holds the blower fan and the cooling coil.
Drain pan
the tray under the coil that catches the water the system removes from the air.
Condensate drain line
the PVC pipe that carries that water to the outside.
Float switch
a safety switch that shuts the system off if water backs up in the pan.
Secondary (auxiliary) drain pan
a larger backup pan placed under units in attics or above living space, with its own float switch, to catch overflow.
