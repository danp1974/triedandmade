My Heat Isn't Working — Check These 7 Things Before You Call a Tech

June 6, 2026Before You Call, Checklist, Heating

Heat not working? 7 checks for Florida heat pumps, electric strip heat, and gas furnaces before you call a tech.

Before You Start — Please Read

This post is here to help you understand your system. It is general information — not professional advice for your exact equipment.

  • You do this at your own risk. If any step does not feel safe, stop and call a licensed pro.
  • Every system is different. What is true for most homes may not be true for yours.
  • Codes change and differ by area. Any code mentioned here may have been updated, or may not apply in your county. Check with your local building department.
  • Leave gas, refrigerant, and major electrical work to licensed professionals. In Florida, this work is often required by law to be done by a licensed tech.
  • If you ever smell gas, do not touch any switch. Leave the house right away and call your gas company or 911 from outside.

Yes, Florida gets cold — and when it does, we're usually not prepared for it. A heating system that hasn't run in 6 or 7 months has a way of acting up right when you need it. The good news is that a lot of "my heat isn't working" calls are caused by something simple. Before you pay a dispatch fee, run through this checklist. It takes about 15 minutes and covers the most common causes I saw over 20 years in the field.

Important — know what type of system you have first

Florida homes typically use one of three heating setups: a heat pump (looks like a standard AC unit, does both heating and cooling), an air handler with electric strip heat (no outdoor unit needed for heat), or a gas furnace (less common in Florida, but some homes have them). The checklist below covers all three, but a few steps are specific to one type — those are noted.

The 7-Step Checklist

1

Check the thermostat — all of it

Set the mode to HEAT (not Cool, not Auto). Set the fan to Auto. Set the temperature setpoint at least 3–4 degrees above the current room temperature — just being "above" isn't always enough to trigger the system. Check the thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered; a weak battery can cause erratic behavior including a system that won't respond to calls for heat. If you have a smart thermostat, check the app for any error codes or lockout messages.

2

Check the circuit breakers

Same as with cooling — your system has two breakers. Both need to be fully on for the system to heat. Find your panel and look for any breaker in the middle or OFF position. Reset by pushing fully off, then back on. If you have a gas furnace, also check that the furnace power switch (looks like a light switch, usually on the wall near the unit or on the unit itself) is in the ON position — these get bumped off surprisingly often.

3

Check the air filter

A clogged filter restricts the airflow your system needs to heat the house just as much as it hurts cooling. If your system has been running with a dirty filter since last cooling season (which is very common), replace it before anything else. Electric strip heat in particular can overheat and trip a high-limit safety switch if airflow is restricted — the system shuts itself off as a safety measure and won't come back on until it cools down, and sometimes not until the limit switch is manually reset.

4

Check the outdoor unit (heat pump owners only)

If you have a heat pump, the outdoor unit runs in heating mode too — it pulls heat energy from the outside air even when it's cold. Check that the unit is running and the fan is spinning. It's normal for the unit to have some frost on it in cold weather — the system goes into a defrost cycle periodically to clear this. What's not normal: a unit completely encased in ice with no defrost cycle running, or a unit that's not running at all while the thermostat is calling for heat.

5

Check all vents are open and unblocked

Same principle as cooling — the system needs return air coming in and supply air going out with no major restrictions. If you closed vents over the summer in rooms you weren't using, open them back up. Check that no furniture has been moved over return vents since last winter.

6

Check the gas supply (gas furnace owners only)

Gas carries real risk, so keep your checks simple and safe. Confirm the furnace power switch (the light-switch-style switch on or near the unit) is ON, and that the thermostat is set to Heat and calling for heat. That is as far as you should go. Do not open the gas valve, relight a pilot light, or take apart the burner assembly — leave anything involving the gas itself to a licensed tech. If you ever smell gas or a rotten-egg odor, do NOT touch any switch or electrical device: leave the house immediately and call your gas company or 911 from outside. If the furnace will not light and you have confirmed the power switch is on, stop here and call a licensed tech.

7

Give the system a full restart

Turn the thermostat off. Go to the breaker panel and shut off both AC/heat breakers. Wait a full 5 minutes. Turn the breakers back on, then set the thermostat to Heat with the setpoint well above room temperature. Heat pumps and electric heat systems can take 5–10 minutes before you feel warm air at the vents — give it time before concluding it's not working.

Why Does Florida Heat Feel Weak?

One complaint I heard constantly during cold snaps: "it's blowing air but it doesn't feel very warm." There are two main reasons for this:

Heat pump output temperature: A heat pump typically delivers air at 90–100°F — warm, but noticeably cooler than the 120–130°F you'd feel from a gas furnace. This is normal. If your heat pump is working correctly and maintaining the target temperature, that mildly warm air is doing its job. The system is designed to run longer at lower output rather than blast short bursts of very hot air.

Emergency or auxiliary heat: Most Florida heat pumps include a backup electric strip heater, and that backup is effectively required by the way the energy code sizes equipment. Florida's energy code (FBC — Energy Conservation, Section R403.7.1.2.1) requires a heat pump to be sized to the home's cooling load — not its heating load. Because the design heating load is often larger than what that cooling-sized heat pump can deliver on the coldest days, supplemental electric strip heat is needed to meet the heating load the code still requires the system to satisfy. A separate section (R403.1.3) then requires controls that keep the strip heat from running unless the heat pump can't meet the load, the system is in defrost, or a component has failed — which is why your aux heat only comes on when it is truly needed. You may see "Aux Heat" or "Em Heat" on your thermostat display. Aux heat is normal and automatic. Emergency heat (Em Heat) mode bypasses the heat pump entirely and runs only the strip heat — this is much more expensive to operate and should only be used as a last resort if the outdoor unit has failed.

When to Call a Tech

🛑 Call a licensed tech if
  • The system went through the full checklist and still produces no heat
  • You have a gas furnace that won't ignite after the gas is confirmed on
  • The outdoor heat pump unit is completely iced over and not running a defrost cycle
  • You smell gas near the furnace — leave the house and call your gas utility immediately
  • A breaker keeps tripping after you reset it
  • The high-limit switch on an electric air handler keeps tripping (usually a separate red reset button on the unit)

A Florida Note on Cold Snaps

Our HVAC systems are designed for the Florida heat, not cold. When temperatures drop into the 30s — which does happen in North and Central Florida — heat pumps that have worked fine for years can struggle to keep up. This isn't a failure; it's the system working at the edge of its design range. If you know a cold snap is coming, run the heat a few degrees higher the night before to pre-condition the house. It's much easier to maintain a target temperature than to try to recover from 58 degrees inside on a cold morning.

If you find your heat pump consistently can't keep up with Florida cold snaps, a conversation with a licensed tech about adding supplemental strip heat capacity may be worth having.

Common Terms Used in This Guide

Heat pump
an outdoor unit that both cools and heats by moving heat; in winter it pulls heat from the outdoor air.
Air handler
the indoor unit with the blower fan and, often, the backup heat.
Electric strip heat
electric heating elements in the air handler used as backup or supplemental heat.
Auxiliary heat (Aux)
the strip heat turning on automatically to help the heat pump when it cannot keep up.
Emergency heat (Em)
a manual mode that runs only the strip heat and bypasses the heat pump; expensive, and a last resort.
Defrost cycle
when the heat pump briefly reverses to melt frost off the outdoor coil in cold weather.
High-limit switch
a safety switch that shuts the heat off if the air handler gets too hot from restricted airflow.
